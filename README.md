id="h.sbksakk5p4b4"><span class="c30 c23">Philosophy</span></h1><p class="c1 c8"><span class="c0"></span></p><p class="c1"><span class="c5">Highlander</span></p><p class="c1"><span class="c10">Card games are at their most interesting in the deck builder. By requiring that players fill their decks with 60 unique cards, highlander brewers are rewarded for their knowledge of the card pool. Singleton formats require a deep understanding of what makes strategies synergize with one another, and the feeling of seeing creative ideas come together into one coherent </span><span class="c10">gameplan</span><span class="c0">&nbsp;is what makes Yugioh so satisfying.</span></p><p class="c1 c8"><span class="c0"></span></p><p class="c1"><span class="c5">Deck Master</span></p><p class="c1"><span class="c0">One of the worst feelings in card games is when you lose the duel from the opening hand because your opponent opened their engine and you didn&rsquo;t. Deck Masters help both players to access their engine&rsquo;s starter from the get go. With Deck Masters, more games feature both duelist&rsquo;s strategies duking it out to see which is more powerful.</span></p><p class="c1 c8"><span class="c0"></span></p><p class="c1"><span class="c5">Summon Limit</span></p><p class="c1"><span class="c0">Modern Yugioh is so powercrept that it hardly resembles the game it once was. By capping the number of summons a player can perform in a turn, the game slows down, and the solitaire-like turns of modern Yugioh are replaced with more grindy, high-turn-count duels.</span></p><p class="c1 c8"><span class="c0"></span></p><p class="c1"><span class="c0">By tying the summon limit to Life Points, the pace of the game gradually accelerates as the duel progresses. Life Point management, which is usually irrelevant so long as you don&rsquo;t let them hit 0, is now a relevant skill. The decision to pay Life Points for the cost of game mechanics like Nightfall, or for powerful spells like Instant Fusion, now carry actual weight.</span></p><p class="c1 c8"><span class="c0"></span></p><p class="c1"><span class="c5">Nightfall</span></p><p class="c1"><span class="c0">Because of the summon limit, both players are always playing under a universal floodgate. Once one player establishes a board or begins to snowball, duels can quickly become insurmountable as the opponent needs to mount a comeback against a summon limit and the opponent&rsquo;s disruptions. Nightfall gives players a way to flip monsters with Quick-Effect disruptions face down, helping to create back-and-forth duels.</span></p><p class="c1 c8"><span class="c0"></span></p><p class="c1"><span class="c5">Conclusion</span></p><p class="c1"><span class="c10">TRH&rsquo;s rules are all geared toward fixing the core issues with Trinity Format. Trinity struggles to balance for modern Yugioh&rsquo;s increasingly powerful boss monsters and floodgates, so Nightfall has been added to provide counterplay. Trinity games are too often decided by which player starts with access to their engine, so Deck Masters have been added to improve consistency. Trinity Format struggles with </span><span class="c10">players snowballing</span><span class="c0">&nbsp;advantage too quickly, so the summon limit now starts smaller and increases gradually as the game progresses.</span></p><p class="c1 c8"><span class="c0"></span></p><table class="c20"><tr class="c29"><td class="c19" colspan="1" rowspan="1"><p class="c26 c8"><span
